Text A

Real Garden Park – General Visitor Information

Real Garden Park is one of the city's most beloved green spaces, covering over 45 hectares of landscaped gardens, woodland walks, and ornamental water features. The park is open to the public every day of the year, including public holidays, from 6:00 am to 9:00 pm during summer months (April–September) and from 7:00 am to 6:00 pm during winter months (October–March).

Admission to the park is free of charge for all visitors. However, entry to the Botanical Glasshouse and the Heritage Rose Collection requires a ticket, available at the park's main entrance kiosk or online. Adult tickets cost £6.50 and concession tickets (for students, seniors aged 60+, and registered disabled visitors) cost £4.00. Children under 12 are admitted free when accompanied by a paying adult.

Dogs are welcome in most areas of the park but must be kept on a lead at all times in the Sensory Garden and the Children's Play Zone. Dog waste bags are available free of charge at all four park entrances. Please note: dogs are not permitted in the Botanical Glasshouse or the café seating area.

Cycling is permitted along designated cycle tracks only. Cyclists must give way to pedestrians at all times. Cycling is prohibited in the formal garden areas, on pathways adjacent to the pond, and within 50 metres of any children's play equipment.

Text B

Real Garden Park – Upcoming Events & Activities

Saturday Garden Walks (Every Saturday, 10:00 am – 11:30 am)
Join our qualified horticulturists for a free guided walk around the park's seasonal highlights. No booking required. Meet at the Clock Tower near the South Gate. Suitable for all ages. Walks take place in all weather conditions.

Junior Gardeners Club (Every Sunday, 2:00 pm – 4:00 pm)
A hands-on gardening programme for children aged 5–12. Activities include planting, composting, and wildlife identification. Places are limited to 15 children per session. Pre-booking is essential and can be done via the park website. A small materials fee of £2 per child per session applies.

Sunset Yoga in the Park (Fridays, June–August, 6:30 pm – 7:30 pm)
Outdoor yoga sessions held on the Great Lawn. Suitable for beginners and experienced practitioners. Participants should bring their own mat. Cost: £8 per session or £50 for the full summer season pass (10 sessions). Book online at the park website.

Photography Exhibition: "A Year in the Garden" (Now – 30th September)
An outdoor display of 60 large-format photographs taken throughout the seasons at Real Garden Park. Free admission. Displayed along the Central Promenade between the fountain and the heritage bridge.

Text A – Staff Induction Handbook (Extract)

Welcome to the Real Garden Park Team

As a member of staff at Real Garden Park, you are expected to uphold the park's values of accessibility, environmental stewardship, and outstanding visitor experience. All new employees must complete a mandatory two-day induction programme before commencing any public-facing duties. During this induction, you will receive training in park safety procedures, visitor engagement, and first aid basics.

Working Hours and Shifts: The park operates on a rota system. Shift patterns are issued four weeks in advance and posted on the staff portal. If you are unable to attend a shift, you must notify your line manager at least 48 hours in advance, except in cases of genuine emergency. Failure to do so may result in a formal warning under the park's attendance management policy.

Uniforms: All customer-facing staff must wear the park uniform (green polo shirt, black trousers/skirt, and name badge) during all working hours. Uniforms are provided free of charge for the first two years of employment. After two years, replacement items are available at a subsidised rate through the HR department. Personal modifications to the uniform (including badges, pins, or accessories) are not permitted unless medically necessary.

Breaks: Staff working a shift of 4 hours or more are entitled to one 20-minute paid break. Staff working 7 hours or more are entitled to one 20-minute paid break and one 30-minute unpaid lunch break. Break times are coordinated by the shift supervisor to ensure adequate coverage at all times.

Text B – Staff Notice

End-of-Season Staff Review & Training Day – Important Notice

All permanent and seasonal staff are required to attend the annual End-of-Season Review and Training Day on Saturday, 5th October. The event will be held at the Park Visitor Centre from 9:00 am to 4:30 pm.

The morning session (9:00 am – 12:30 pm) will include a review of visitor feedback, departmental performance updates, and a presentation from the Park Director on plans for the upcoming season. Attendance at the morning session is compulsory for all staff.

The afternoon session (1:30 pm – 4:30 pm) will consist of three optional workshops: Advanced Plant Care, Visitor Accessibility Training, and Digital Communication for Park Staff. Staff should pre-select their preferred workshop via the staff portal by 25th September. Places are allocated on a first-come, first-served basis.

Lunch will be provided free of charge between 12:30 pm and 1:30 pm. Staff who travel more than 15 miles to attend may claim travel expenses through the Finance Department within 14 days of the event. Claims submitted after this period will not be processed.

Please note that the park will be closed to the public on this date. Skeleton staff for essential maintenance will be arranged separately by department heads.

Section 3 · General Interest
The Urban Green Revolution: How City Parks Transformed Public Life
Read the passage and answer Questions 28–40 · Long text, general interest

A. For much of history, urban life and nature existed in opposition. Cities were places of commerce, density, and noise — not greenery and birdsong. Yet over the past two centuries, a quiet revolution transformed this relationship. The deliberate creation of public parks reshaped not only the physical landscape of cities but also the social and psychological lives of their inhabitants. Today, urban green spaces are recognised as essential infrastructure, as vital to a healthy city as roads, hospitals, or schools.

B. The modern public park as we know it emerged in the nineteenth century, largely as a response to the rapid and often chaotic industrialisation of European and North American cities. As factories multiplied and urban populations swelled, conditions in working-class districts became increasingly grim. Streets were crowded, housing was cramped, and air quality deteriorated sharply. Social reformers, physicians, and politicians began to argue that access to open air and greenery was not a luxury but a public health necessity. In Britain, reformers such as Joseph Paxton championed the creation of parks for the urban poor, leading to the development of spaces such as Birkenhead Park in 1847 — a park so influential that American landscape architect Frederick Law Olmsted visited it and later cited it as the inspiration for New York's Central Park.

C. The design philosophies behind these early parks varied considerably. Some, like Birkenhead and Central Park, embraced a naturalistic approach — winding pathways, undulating lawns, and carefully positioned trees that gave the impression of natural countryside within an urban setting. Others adopted a more formal, geometric style, with symmetrical flowerbeds, straight avenues, and ornamental fountains. In France, the grand redesign of Paris under Baron Haussmann incorporated parks as deliberate civic showpieces — places that expressed municipal pride and civic order rather than wild nature. These different approaches reflected deeper debates about the purpose of public space: Was it to provide escape and tranquillity? To project civic power? Or simply to improve sanitation and public health?

D. As the twentieth century progressed, the role of the city park evolved in response to changing social attitudes and urban needs. During the post-war period, many parks in western cities fell into neglect. Reduced municipal budgets, shifting priorities, and, in some cases, the departure of affluent residents to suburbs left parks underfunded and underused. Vandalism increased, maintenance declined, and many parks gained reputations as unsafe places. In some American cities, this reached a crisis point by the 1970s and 1980s, with parks becoming associated with crime and social disorder.

E. The revitalisation of urban parks from the late 1980s onward was driven by a combination of factors. Changing attitudes toward the environment, increased awareness of physical and mental health benefits, and growing evidence linking green space to well-being all played a role. Cities began to invest once again in parks as strategic assets rather than costly liabilities. In New York, the formation of the Central Park Conservancy in 1980 marked a turning point: a public-private partnership model that transformed a neglected green space into one of the world's most visited urban parks. Similar models were replicated in cities across Europe and beyond.

F. The scientific case for urban green space has strengthened considerably in recent decades. Research consistently shows that access to parks and green areas is associated with lower levels of stress, reduced symptoms of anxiety and depression, and improved physical health outcomes through increased exercise. Studies in the United Kingdom have found that people living within 300 metres of a quality green space report significantly higher levels of wellbeing than those without such access. Moreover, green spaces provide broader ecological benefits: trees in parks absorb carbon dioxide, reduce the urban heat island effect, manage stormwater, and provide habitat for urban wildlife. A study in Tokyo estimated that the city's parks prevent temperatures from rising by up to 2°C in surrounding areas during summer months.

G. Yet despite this growing body of evidence, urban green spaces remain unevenly distributed. In many cities, the most affluent neighbourhoods have significantly better access to high-quality parks than poorer areas. This phenomenon — sometimes referred to as "green gentrification" — raises difficult questions. When parks improve, surrounding property values tend to rise, often pushing out the lower-income communities that the parks were originally meant to serve. Urban planners and policymakers are increasingly grappling with how to expand and improve green space access equitably, without triggering cycles of displacement.

H. Looking ahead, the future of urban parks is likely to be shaped by climate change, growing urban populations, and the integration of new technologies. "Biophilic design" — an approach that seeks to embed nature throughout the built environment, not just in designated parks — is gaining traction. Green roofs, living walls, urban forests, and pocket parks are being incorporated into city planning in ways that were unimaginable a century ago. Some planners speak of transforming entire cities into green infrastructure, blurring the boundary between park and city itself. Whatever form they take, it seems clear that green spaces will continue to occupy a central place in the story of how cities evolve — and how their residents live.
